Experience

Co-op placements where I've applied engineering fundamentals to real hardware and real constraints.

Engineering Assistant

TRIUMF — ARIEL Facility

January 2026 – April 2026 Vancouver, BC

At TRIUMF, Canada's national particle accelerator centre, I worked on the auxiliary gas systems team, designing equipment that manages the radioactive decay gases produced by the ARIEL target stations and directs them safely into radioactive decay storage tanks.

What made the experience special was the scale of it. I was one small part of a huge project, contributing to systems I could never have built on my own, supporting world-class research. It gave me a real sense of what large-scale engineering can achieve when expertise from around the world comes together and a clearer picture of the kind of work I want to be part of.

The biggest thing I took away was learning how engineering actually works inside a large organization. My design didn't exist on its own, it had to stay traceable and synchronized with everyone else's. I managed bills of materials within a PDM system and coordinated with engineers and technicians to maintain configuration control across iterative frame revisions, so that every change was documented and the whole team was on the same page. I also worked alongside the technicians who fabricated the frame, talking with them throughout the design process about how they would build it and whether anything in my design would restrict them. I learned the value of communication and working within PDM and BOM systems, keeping my work traceable and coordinating across different teams of people.

Mechanical Engineering Co-op

BC Hydro — Site C Clean Energy Project

May 2024 – August 2024 Fort St. John, BC

I spent my BC Hydro co-op at Site C, a major hydroelectric dam under construction in northern British Columbia. I lived on camp near the project and worked alongside the people physically building it: contractors, technicians, and electricians, which gave me a perspective most engineering students never get: a firsthand feel for what it's actually like to construct the designs that come off an engineer's desk.

My work was centred on design review and field verification. I reviewed and redlined P&IDs and mechanical layouts, recommending changes to improve constructability, and I was regularly on site watching mechanical systems get installed and tested while noting discrepancies between the drawings and the build and feeding them back so they could be resolved. I also helped translate RFIs and field change forms into practical design revisions as construction progressed.

What I took away was an eye for constructability. Being on site every day, alongside the trades, I started to see designs the way the people building them do — where a layout would be awkward to assemble, where a drawing left too much open to interpretation, where something looked fine on paper but was a problem in the field. I learned that a good design isn't just one that works, but one that can actually be built cleanly by the people on the ground.

That perspective carries into any design role: I now think about the build and the people doing it while a design is still on the screen, which means catching constructability problems early instead of discovering them during installation.
